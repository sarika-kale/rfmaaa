    What is RFM analysis? Why RFM for Segmentation?

    Recency, Frequency, and Monetary (RFM) analysis is used to represent customer behavior characteristics. RFM models have been used in direct marketing for more than 30 years. Research strongly suggests that past purchases of consumers are better predictors of their future purchase behavior than demographics. RFM Analysis divides consumers into segments based on the value of their purchases (Monetary), the frequency of their purchases (Frequency), and the time since last purchase (Recency). Given these segments, businesses can identify which customers are more important to their business, and and efficiently target them based on their behaviour patterns. This is explained further under section Data Visualization.

    RFM Segments

    Subjects are coded on each of the three dimensions. A common approach is to have five cells for each of the three measures, yielding a total of 125 combinations, each of which can be associated with a probability of a positive response to the marketing campaign. Bult and Wansbeek (1995) defined RFM as: 1. Recency (R): the period since the last purchase; a lower value corresponds to a higher probability of the customer ́s making a repeat purchase 2. Frequency (F): number of purchases made within a certain period; higher frequency indicates greater loyalty 3. Monetary (M): the money spent during a certain period; a higher value indicates that the company should focus more on that customer

    This tree map shows the hierarchy of RFM Segments based on Total Sales Value per segment. The tree map is as expected, the largest block is assigned to "Champions" which has the most total sales, whereas the smallest block is assigned to "Lost" which has the lowest total sales.

    However, we can also construct the map on the basis of Average Order Value, this reveals a different story. The largest block is the "Can't Lose Them", which has the highest Average Order Value of all the segments. These are customers who spend heavily, with moderate frequency, but have not done so recently. Having identified this segment, businesses can try to win them back, with high priority, via renewals or newer products. Customers should also be interviewed to identify why they left. The "At Risk" Segment is similar to "Can't Lose Them" but with spend relatively lesser. This segment can be tended to next.
    Another key segment is "Loyal", a segment that has fairly high total sales and average order value. Given their brand loyalty, businesses can upsell higher value products to this segment. In contrast, for the segment "Lost" businesses can try to reach them out with general, low-cost campaigns, otherwise ignore them. Overall, identifying RFM Segments can help businesses prioritise which consumers to target, and how to best target them based on their purchasing behaviour.
